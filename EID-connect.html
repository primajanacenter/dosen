When Preparedness is Not Enough

The human side of disease surveillance

→ Read the full paper

A healthcare worker sits in a primary health center. A patient arrives with unusual symptoms—fever, fatigue, something that does not quite fit the usual pattern.

At that moment, the surveillance system does not act on its own. There is no automatic trigger, no invisible mechanism that guarantees action. Everything depends on a decision.

Will the case be reported? Investigated further? Or quietly absorbed into routine care?

“Surveillance systems do not fail because of data. They fail because of decisions.”

For years, global health discussions have focused on strengthening systems—better tools, more infrastructure, more training. These efforts matter. But they are not the whole story.

This study reveals something deeper: preparedness alone is not enough. What determines whether surveillance works is not only what healthcare workers know—but whether they are willing to act.

Willingness is shaped by trust, experience, and human connection—not knowledge alone.

Even when knowledge, training, and system factors are combined, they explain only part of the story. The rest lies beyond measurement—in human behavior, perception, and context.

The strongest force is not technical capacity. It is trust.

“When healthcare workers feel trusted by the community, they are more willing to act.”

Surveillance, in this sense, is not merely a technical process. It is relational.

Knowledge remains important. Healthcare workers understand surveillance systems and recognize their importance. But knowledge alone does not guarantee action.

“They know what to do—but that does not mean they will do it.”

Training helps bridge this gap, transforming understanding into practice. Yet even training has limits if it is not grounded in real-world conditions.

Then there is the system itself. Constraints—limited resources, workload pressures, operational challenges—are often seen as barriers.

Yet the findings suggest something unexpected: constraints do not always reduce engagement. In some cases, they strengthen it.

“In imperfect systems, resilience emerges.”

Healthcare workers adapt. They compensate. They find ways to act, even when systems fall short.

A strong surveillance system is not built on infrastructure alone—it is built on trust, relationships, and human resilience.

If we want to strengthen surveillance, we must look beyond systems. We must understand the human conditions that shape action.

Do healthcare workers feel supported? Do they feel trusted? Do they feel capable of acting?

“A resilient system is not just prepared—it is human-centered.”
